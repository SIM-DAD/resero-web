The problem

Encryption stops being
useful the moment you
can’t open the file.

The age file format is small, modern, and well-specified, but the official tooling is command-line only. That’s fine for sysadmins. It’s less fine for a journalist on a fresh laptop, a researcher with an old archive, or a SIM DAD customer who wants their library back without reinstalling the app that wrote it. Resero is the missing GUI: open the file, hand it the right identity, get plaintext.

What Resero does

Drag, drop, decrypt.

One window. Three ways to give it your identity. Single files or a whole folder.

01

Three identity modes

Passphrase (scrypt), key file (X25519 secret key from a .txt), or auto-detect from your OS keychain for Ibis, Lector, and TASS libraries. The right mode is usually obvious from the file you dragged in.

02

Bulk and folder watch

Drop a folder; Resero walks it and decrypts every age file in place. Folder watch mode keeps an eye on a directory and decrypts new ciphertext as it arrives, useful for scripted backup recovery.

03

Cross-platform, cross-OS

age files are byte-identical across Windows, macOS, and Linux. Only the keystore differs. Export your X25519 secret from the OS where you encrypted, import it on a fresh machine, decrypt the same files. Recovery without reinstalling the original product.

04

MIT-licensed core

The decryption module is published on GitHub under the MIT license, separately from the GUI. Audit it, fork it, ship it inside another tool. The GUI is a thin wrapper; the cryptography is not proprietary.

Why it’s free

No-lock-in is a claim.
Resero is the proof.

Every SIM DAD product encrypts at rest. We could ask you to take that on faith, or we could ship the recovery tool ourselves and license it MIT. Resero exists so the encryption story isn’t "trust us." It’s "here is the source, here is the binary, here is the file format spec it conforms to, decrypt your own data without us in the loop."
